Spousal & Survivor Benefits
Up to 50% of spouse's benefit · 100% survivor benefit

Spousal Benefits

  • A spouse can receive up to 50% of the worker's FRA benefit — whichever is higher between their own or spousal benefit
  • Must be at least 62 to claim spousal benefit (or any age if caring for a child under 16)
  • Spousal benefit is permanently reduced if claimed before FRA
  • Divorced spouses who were married 10+ years may also qualify — even if the ex-spouse has remarried

Survivor Benefits

  • Widow or widower can receive up to 100% of the deceased spouse's benefit
  • Survivor benefits available as early as age 60 (50 if disabled)
  • Strategy: claim your own SS at 62 and switch to survivor benefit at FRA if it's higher

Break-Even Age Explained
When delaying SS pays off

Break-even is the age at which the total lifetime benefits from waiting exceed what you would have received by claiming early. General break-even ages:

  • Claiming at 62 vs. 67: break-even around age 78–80
  • Claiming at 67 vs. 70: break-even around age 82–84
  • If you expect to live past 80–82, delaying SS is usually beneficial
  • Health, marital status, and need for income now all affect the decision

Use the CFPB's free claiming tool: consumerfinance.gov/retirement/before-you-claim

Working While Collecting SS
Earnings limits · benefit recalculation rules
  • Under FRA: $1 withheld for every $2 earned above $22,320/yr (2025)
  • Year of FRA: $1 withheld for every $3 earned above $59,520/yr (2025)
  • At or after FRA: No earnings limit — earn as much as you want
  • Withheld benefits are not lost — SSA recalculates and increases your benefit at FRA
  • Working longer may increase your benefit if new earnings replace lower-earning years in your 35-year record
SSA.gov — Working While Retired
Windfall Elimination & GPO
Affects government employees with pensions

Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) reduces SS benefits for workers who receive a pension from a job not covered by SS (e.g. some state/local government jobs, some non-U.S. employment). The reduction can be up to half your pension amount but not more than half your SS benefit.

Government Pension Offset (GPO) reduces spousal/survivor SS benefits for those who receive a government pension not covered by SS. The reduction is 2/3 of your government pension.

Note: The Social Security Fairness Act (2024) repealed WEP and GPO — retroactive benefit increases expected for affected retirees. Check SSA.gov for updates.

Roth Conversion Strategy
Converting traditional funds to Roth to reduce future taxes

A Roth conversion moves money from a traditional IRA or 401(k) to a Roth IRA. You pay income tax now on the converted amount, but future withdrawals are tax-free.

Best Times to Convert

  • Years when your income is temporarily lower (early retirement, gap years)
  • Before Social Security and RMDs begin — typically ages 60–72
  • When the market is down — you convert fewer dollars at a lower tax cost
  • When you expect your tax rate to be higher in the future

Key Considerations

  • Conversions are taxable in the year completed — may push you into a higher bracket
  • Can increase Medicare Part B/D premiums via IRMAA surcharges 2 years later
  • Roth accounts have no RMDs during the owner's lifetime — major advantage
  • 5-year rule: converted funds must remain 5 years before tax-free withdrawal

Early Withdrawal Exceptions (Before 59½)
Avoid the 10% penalty in these situations

The 10% early withdrawal penalty is waived in these circumstances (income tax still applies):

  • Rule of 55: Separated from employer at age 55+ — can withdraw from that employer's plan penalty-free
  • SEPP / 72(t): Substantially Equal Periodic Payments — structured withdrawals over at least 5 years
  • Total & permanent disability
  • Death — beneficiary withdrawals
  • Unreimbursed medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI
  • Health insurance premiums while unemployed (IRA only)
  • First-time home purchase up to $10,000 (IRA only)
  • Qualified higher education expenses (IRA only)
  • Birth or adoption: up to $5,000 per event (SECURE 2.0)
IRS.gov — Early Distribution Exceptions
Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)
Rules, deadlines, and calculation methods
  • Start age: 73 (per SECURE 2.0 Act, effective 2023). Rises to 75 for those born after 1959.
  • First RMD deadline: April 1 of the year after you turn 73 — but taking two distributions in one year can push you into a higher tax bracket
  • Subsequent RMDs: December 31 of each year
  • Calculation: Account balance ÷ IRS life expectancy factor from Uniform Lifetime Table
  • RMDs apply to: Traditional 401(k), Traditional IRA, SEP IRA, SIMPLE IRA, 403(b), 457(b)
  • RMDs do NOT apply to: Roth IRA during owner's lifetime
  • Still working exception: If still employed at 73 and own less than 5% of the company, can delay 401(k) RMDs from current employer's plan

The Four Parts of Medicare — What Each Covers
  • Part A — Hospital Insurance: Inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice, some home health. Free if you worked 40+ quarters (10 years). Premium of $518/mo if fewer than 30 quarters worked.
  • Part B — Medical Insurance: Outpatient care, doctor visits, preventive services, durable medical equipment, mental health. Premium: $185/mo (2025 standard). Higher-income earners pay IRMAA surcharges.
  • Part C — Medicare Advantage: Private insurance plans that cover everything in Parts A and B, usually include Part D, and often add dental, vision, and hearing. Offered by private insurers approved by Medicare.
  • Part D — Prescription Drug Coverage: Covers prescription medications. Purchased separately or bundled with Part C. Average premium ~$46/mo in 2025. New $2,000 annual out-of-pocket cap as of 2025.
  • Medigap (Supplemental Insurance): Private plans that pay costs original Medicare doesn't cover — deductibles, copayments, coinsurance. Best purchased at 65 during open enrollment for guaranteed coverage.

Medicare Enrollment Windows
Miss these and face permanent late penalties
  • Initial Enrollment Period (IEP): 7-month window — 3 months before your 65th birthday month, your birthday month, and 3 months after. Best to enroll in month 1–3 to avoid delayed coverage.
  • Special Enrollment Period (SEP): 8-month window after losing employer health coverage or stopping work. Covers those still working at 65 with employer insurance.
  • General Enrollment Period (GEP): January 1 – March 31 each year. Late penalty applies — 10% per 12-month period you were eligible but not enrolled.
  • Open Enrollment (Part C & D): October 15 – December 7 each year. Switch plans, add/drop coverage. Changes take effect January 1.
  • Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment: January 1 – March 31. Switch to original Medicare or change Advantage plans once.

Long-Term Care Planning
Medicare does NOT cover custodial care

Medicare covers short-term skilled nursing facility care (up to 100 days) but does NOT cover ongoing custodial care (help with bathing, dressing, eating). Options to cover this gap:

  • Long-Term Care (LTC) Insurance: Best purchased at ages 55–65 when premiums are lowest. Covers nursing home, assisted living, and in-home care. Average cost: $2,500–$4,000/yr at age 60.
  • Hybrid Life/LTC Policies: Life insurance with an LTC rider — unused LTC benefit passes to heirs as a death benefit.
  • Medicaid: Covers long-term care for those who qualify financially — typically requires spending down assets. Rules vary by state.
  • HSA Funds: Can be used tax-free to pay LTC insurance premiums (subject to age-based limits).
  • Average Costs 2025: Nursing home $95,000–$115,000/yr · Assisted living $54,000/yr · Home health aide $30/hr (Genworth Cost of Care Survey)

Federal Tax Benefits for Seniors
  • Higher Standard Deduction: Additional $1,950 (single) / $1,550 per spouse (married) for those 65+ in 2025. Source: IRS Publication 554
  • Credit for the Elderly or Disabled: Non-refundable credit of $3,750–$7,500 for qualifying low-income seniors. IRS Schedule R
  • SS Taxation Thresholds: SS benefits taxable only if combined income exceeds $25,000 (single) or $32,000 (married). Up to 85% may be taxable above $34,000/$44,000.
  • Medical Deduction: Deduct qualified medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI. Source: IRS Publication 502
  • Free Tax Help: AARP Tax-Aide (aarp.org/taxaide) and IRS VITA/TCE programs offer free tax preparation for seniors nationwide.

The 4% Withdrawal Rule
How much you can safely withdraw each year

The 4% rule — from the 1994 "Trinity Study" — suggests withdrawing 4% of your portfolio in year one, then adjusting for inflation each year. It was designed to sustain a portfolio for 30 years with a 90%+ success rate.

  • Example: $1,000,000 portfolio → $40,000/yr ($3,333/mo) initial withdrawal
  • Portfolio needed to retire: Multiply desired annual income by 25 (i.e. $60,000/yr needs $1,500,000)
  • Limitations: Based on 60/40 stock/bond portfolios; today's low bond yields may require a 3–3.5% rate
  • Sequence of returns risk: Poor returns early in retirement can deplete a portfolio faster — consider a cash buffer of 1–2 years expenses
  • Update: Some researchers now suggest 3.3%–3.8% for 40+ year retirements. Consult a CFP for personalized guidance.
CFPB Retirement Tools
Health Savings Account (HSA) Strategy
Triple tax-advantaged account for healthcare costs

An HSA is one of the most powerful retirement tools — triple tax-advantaged: contributions are pre-tax, growth is tax-free, and qualified withdrawals are tax-free.

  • 2025 Contribution Limits: $4,300 (self-only) / $8,550 (family) — +$1,000 catch-up at 55+
  • Eligible only with a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)
  • Power strategy: Pay medical costs out-of-pocket now, keep receipts, reimburse yourself tax-free in retirement — no time limit on reimbursement
  • After 65: Withdraw for any reason (pay income tax, like a Traditional IRA) — still tax-free for qualified medical
  • Medicare enrollees cannot contribute — stop contributions 6 months before enrolling in Medicare Part A
  • Medicare premiums (Part B, D, Advantage) are qualified HSA medical expenses

Estate Planning Essentials
Documents every retiree needs in place
  • Will: Directs asset distribution. Without one, state intestacy laws decide — often not what you intended. Update after major life events.
  • Durable Power of Attorney: Authorizes someone to manage your finances if incapacitated. "Durable" means it survives incapacity.
  • Healthcare Proxy / Medical POA: Names someone to make medical decisions if you cannot.
  • Living Will / Advance Directive: Documents your wishes for end-of-life care, CPR, ventilators, feeding tubes.
  • Beneficiary Designations: Review on ALL accounts (401k, IRA, insurance, bank). They override your will and take immediate effect at death.
  • Revocable Living Trust: Avoids probate, maintains privacy, controls distribution timing. Consider if estate >$200,000 or you own property in multiple states.
  • 2025 Federal Estate Tax Exemption: $13.61 million per person — most estates owe no federal estate tax.
CFPB — Estate Planning Guide
Retirement Income Strategy & Tax Planning
Which accounts to draw from first

Optimal Withdrawal Order (general rule):

  • 1. RMDs first — legally required by Dec 31 each year (age 73+)
  • 2. Taxable brokerage accounts — capital gains rates often lower than ordinary income
  • 3. Traditional IRA / 401(k) — reduces future RMD amounts
  • 4. Roth IRA / Roth 401(k) last — tax-free, no RMDs, best for legacy

Key tax traps to avoid:

  • Up to 85% of SS benefits are taxable if combined income exceeds $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (married)
  • IRMAA surcharges on Medicare Part B/D kick in at $103,000+ income (single) — based on income 2 years prior
  • Taking large 401(k) withdrawals can push SS benefits into taxable range — plan withdrawal timing carefully

10 Best-Practice Tips for Retirement Planning
  • Delay Social Security as long as financially feasible — each year past FRA adds 8%
  • Max out contributions in your final working years, especially 50+ catch-up amounts
  • Pay off all high-interest debt before retiring — it compounds against you in retirement
  • Build 1–2 years of living expenses in cash to avoid selling investments in a downturn
  • Review and update all beneficiary designations every 2–3 years and after life events
  • Enroll in Medicare at 65 even if covered elsewhere — late enrollment penalties are permanent
  • Consider long-term care insurance in your late 50s–early 60s when premiums are lowest
  • Do Roth conversions in low-income years before Social Security and RMDs begin
  • Plan for Social Security to cover your fixed expenses — it is inflation-adjusted and lasts for life
  • Work with a fee-only CFP (Certified Financial Planner) — find one at cfp.net or napfa.org
